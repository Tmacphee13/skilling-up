Demux, index hopping & the i5 flip

The one lesson that lands squarely on your daily job — now with mechanism underneath it.

You run this every day. The goal here isn't to teach you the commands — it's to give the three things you handle downstream a mechanistic why, so a bad run becomes a diagnosis instead of a mystery: the Undetermined pile, index hopping, and the i5 reverse-complement flip. Everything below builds on the four-reads sequence from Lesson 2.

What demux actually decides

After the run, every cluster has an i7 and i5 read (the I1/I2 bursts). Demux does one thing per cluster: compare its observed index pair against the sample sheet, and route the read to whichever sample matches — or to Undetermined if none does. Two rules do all the work:

  • Both indexes must match. A dual-indexed read is assigned only if its i7 and its i5 both match the same sample sheet row.
  • Matching allows a small mismatch budget. The default tolerance is 1 mismatch per index. Sequencing isn't perfect, so an index that's off by one base is still assigned — as long as it's still closer to one sample than any other.
Why indexes are designed far apart That 1-mismatch budget only works if no two indexes in the pool are within 2 bases of each other. If two indexes differed by a single base, a one-base error could turn one into the other and misassign the read. This minimum edit distance (Hamming distance) is why index kits use carefully chosen sequences — and why you can't just pool two samples whose indexes look nearly identical. Low index diversity is also why demux (and the base-caller) struggle on low-plex or unbalanced pools.

Index hopping — the mechanism

An index hop is a read that gets mis-labeled with the wrong sample's index. The cause is physical: a pool always contains some free-floating index primers / adapter fragments left over from library prep. On a patterned flow cell, the ExAmp reaction (Lesson 2) is fast and happens in bulk before clustering finishes — so one of those free indexes can anneal to another sample's molecule and get extended by polymerase, stamping it with the wrong barcode. The read is real DNA; only its index tag is a lie.

This is why hopping is a patterned-flow-cell problem (NovaSeq, NextSeq, iSeq) far more than a non-patterned one (MiSeq). It's also why it's worse on low-input or over-amplified libraries — more free adapter floating around.

UDI vs CDI — why the pool design decides your fate

Here's the part that actually protects your data, and it's a design choice made before the run:

Combinatorial (CDI)Unique dual (UDI)
Index reusei7s and i5s reused in a grid of combinationseach i7 paired with one dedicated i5, never reused
A hopped read…may land on a valid i7+i5 comboproduces an i7+i5 pair not in the sheet
Resultsilently misassigned to a real samplecaught → dropped to Undetermined

That's the whole point of UDI: it can't stop the hop, but it converts a silent, invisible contamination into a harmless read in the Undetermined pile — exactly what happened to Read 3 above. UDI drops cross-contamination from ~1% to under 0.01% on ExAmp systems. This is why single-cell, low-input, and any sensitive assay should insist on UDI.

The i5 flip, in full

Now the gotcha you already meet in sample sheets, with its real cause from Lesson 2. The i5 index is read off the original strand on some instruments and off the turnaround-rebuilt strand on others. Those strands are reverse complements, so the same physical i5 is reported two ways:

Forward-strand workflow (i5 as-is)Reverse-complement workflow (i5 flipped)
MiSeq, HiSeq 2000/2500, MiniSeq RapidiSeq 100, MiniSeq (standard), NextSeq 500/550 & 1000/2000, HiSeq 3000/4000/X, NovaSeq 6000 (v1.5), NovaSeq X
Diagnosing it at your desk The signature: near-total Undetermined, but the i7s look fine and the top unknown barcodes are the reverse complements of your i5s. That's a flipped i5 in the sample sheet, not a failed run. Modern planners (BaseSpace, Local Run Manager, DRAGEN) auto-reverse- complement i5 for the instrument if you enter it forward — so hand-built or ported sample sheets are where this bites. First move when Undetermined is huge: open the Top Unknown Barcodes report and check whether the mystery i5s are revcomps of your expected ones.
