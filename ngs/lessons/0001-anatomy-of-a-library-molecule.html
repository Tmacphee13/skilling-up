The anatomy of a library molecule

Where your FASTQ files come from — read from the molecule outward.

You already live at the output end of sequencing: a run finishes, you get R1, R2, sometimes I1/I2 files, an Undetermined pile, and a sample sheet. This lesson works in the other direction. If you understand the single molecule that produced those files, every artifact you handle downstream stops being a convention you memorized and becomes something you can derive.

One tangible win by the end: you'll be able to draw the molecule from memory and point to exactly which segment produces each file demux hands you.

The molecule, end to end

Every fragment your wetlab team hands the sequencer has been dressed up with adapters on both ends. The sample DNA in the middle is the insert — the only part you actually care about biologically. Everything else is machinery. Here it is, 5′ → 3′ along the top strand:

P5flow-cell
i5Index 2
SP1Read 1 primer
INSERTyour sample DNA / cDNA
SP2Read 2 primer
i7Index 1
P7flow-cell
P5 / P7 — grab the flow cell i5 / i7 — sample barcodes SP1 / SP2 — where reading starts insert — the DNA of interest

Three jobs, three kinds of segment:

1 · P5 and P7 — attachment

The flow cell surface is a lawn of two grafted oligos. P5 and P7 are the universal ends of your molecule that hybridize to that lawn. This is how a molecule sticks to the flow cell and seeds a cluster — a clonal copy-cluster bright enough to image. No P5/P7, no cluster, no read. (glossary: cluster, SBS)

2 · i5 and i7 — identity

The two index barcodes say which sample this molecule came from. Because they're baked into the adapter, dozens or hundreds of samples can share one lane and be told apart later. This is the physical basis of multiplexing — and of the demux you run.

3 · SP1 and SP2 — where reading begins

A sequencing primer has to anneal somewhere before synthesis can start. SP1 sits just upstream of the insert on the P5 side and launches Read 1 into the insert. SP2 launches Read 2 from the P7 side, reading the insert from the opposite end. That opposite-ends pair is exactly what paired-end means.

The molecule → your four files

Here's the whole point. Each thing demux hands you is one segment of this molecule read in one direction:

FileSegment readStarted from
R1insert, P5→P7 directionSP1
R2insert, P7→P5 directionSP2
I1i7 barcodeIndex 1 read
I2i5 barcodeIndex 2 read

The instrument reads these as up to four separate bursts of chemistry on the same cluster: Read 1, then Index 1, then Index 2, then Read 2. Demux never "finds" the index inside R1 — the index reads are their own cycles, physically separate from the insert. That's why a run with the wrong index cycle count silently produces garbage indexes and a mountain of Undetermined.

Why this matters at your desk The i5 barcode is read from opposite strands depending on the instrument's chemistry, so the same physical index appears as its reverse complement on some instruments (NovaSeq, NextSeq, iSeq) versus others (MiSeq, HiSeq 2500). When a run demuxes to near-100% Undetermined but the i7s look fine, a flipped i5 in the sample sheet is the usual culprit — not a failed run. You now know why that flip exists: it's the same barcode, read off the other strand. We'll dig into this in a later lesson.
