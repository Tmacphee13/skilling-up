NGS Foundations · Lesson 4

From sample to library

How the wetlab turns a tube of tissue into the adapter-flanked molecule you've been reading — and why DNA-seq, RNA-seq & friends diverge.

So far you've built the finished library molecule (L1), read it (L2), and demuxed it (L3). But that molecule doesn't arrive ready-made — the wetlab builds it. This lesson is library prep: the bench workflow that converts raw sample nucleic acid into sequenceable molecules. Understand its skeleton and you can predict the QC signature of any prep that crosses your desk.

Every prep answers the same three questions

The dizzying catalog of kits is really variations on one recipe. Every library prep must:

  1. Get the nucleic acid of interest — and enrich away what you don't want.
  2. Cut it to the right size — an insert that matches your read length.
  3. Attach the adapters — the P5 / i5 / SP1 … SP2 / i7 / P7 scaffold from Lesson 1.

Different assays answer these differently. That's the whole map.

Two ways to attach adapters

Question 3 splits the field into two dominant chemistries:

Ligation-based (e.g. TruSeq)

  1. Fragment
    Shear or enzymatically cut DNA to ~200–500 bp.
  2. End repair + A-tailing
    Blunt the ragged ends, add a single 3′ A so a T-tailed adapter can pair.
  3. Ligate adapters
    T4 ligase joins adapters to both ends.
  4. PCR
    Amplify and, in many kits, add the indexes.

Robust, works across a wide input range, low bias — the workhorse for WGS and RNA.

Tagmentation (e.g. Nextera)

A hyperactive Tn5 transposase pre-loaded with adapters cuts the DNA and inserts the adapters in a single step — collapsing fragmentation, end-repair, and ligation into one reaction. Minutes instead of hours, tiny input, but more sequence bias and a distinctive insert-size pattern.

Why this matters at your desk This is the origin of the two adapter families you trim against: TruSeq adapters vs Nextera adapters are different sequences because they're attached by different chemistries. When your trimmer auto-detects "Nextera transposase sequence," it's telling you the prep was tagmentation. And insert size is set here, at fragmentation — if the insert is shorter than your read length, sequencing runs off the end into the adapter (adapter read-through), which is exactly the adapter contamination you trim. Short inserts → more trimming. It's not random; it's the prep.

The big divergence: DNA vs RNA

Here's the one that answers your earlier cDNA question. Illumina sequencing reads DNA — RNA can't bind the flow cell or be copied by the SBS polymerase. So an RNA library has extra steps before it rejoins the DNA workflow:

  1. Enrich the RNA you want
    PolyA selection (oligo-dT beads grab mRNA's polyA tail) for gene expression, or rRNA depletion (pull out ribosomal RNA) for total RNA. You must do one: rRNA is ~80–90% of total RNA and would otherwise eat your reads.
  2. Reverse-transcribe → 1st-strand cDNA
    Reverse transcriptase copies RNA into a single strand of cDNA. This is why your cDNA started single-stranded (your L2 question).
  3. 2nd-strand synthesis → ds cDNA
    Make the complementary strand. Now you have double-stranded cDNA…
  4. …now it's just DNA prep
    Fragment (if not already), adapters, PCR — identical to the DNA workflow above.

So DNA-seq and RNA-seq differ only at the front: RNA needs enrichment + reverse transcription to become DNA, then the two converge. WGS, WES, amplicon, and single-cell are all further front-end variations on the same back half.

Strandedness — a free consequence worth knowing

Plain cDNA loses track of which strand the RNA came from. Stranded protocols preserve it by building the 2nd strand with dUTP instead of dTTP, then destroying that uracil-marked strand before amplification — so only the original orientation survives. At your desk this is the -s / strandedness flag in featureCounts/HTSeq and the "reverse/forward" setting in Salmon. Guess it wrong and gene counts crater. Now you know what physically created the signal.
