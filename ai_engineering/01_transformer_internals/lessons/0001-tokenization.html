Tokenization: the model never sees your text

Here is the first surprising fact, and it explains a startling amount of LLM behavior: a language model never sees characters, and it never sees words. Before your prompt reaches the network, it is chopped into tokens and each token is swapped for an integer ID. The model's entire universe is a sequence of these IDs — say [9906, 11, 1917] — and its only job is to predict the next one.

Why this is Lesson 1 You already know a network takes in numbers and does matrix math. The missing piece between "my English sentence" and "numbers a neural net can chew on" is tokenization. Everything else in this course sits on top of it.

What a token actually is

A token is usually a sub-word: bigger than a letter, often smaller than a word. Common words (" the", " and") get their own single token. Rare or novel strings get spelled out from several pieces. The word "tokenization" might split as token + ization; a typo or a random string like "asdf1234" shatters into many tiny fragments.

The complete set of tokens a model knows is its vocabulary — roughly 100,000 entries for GPT-4o-class models. Notice the leading spaces above: " the" (with space) and "the" (without) are different tokens. That detail alone causes a lot of "why did the model do that?" moments.

Where the vocabulary comes from: BPE

Nobody hand-writes 100k tokens. They're learned from a big pile of text by an algorithm called Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE), and the idea is charmingly simple:1

  1. Start with the rawest possible units — individual bytes.
  2. Scan the corpus and find the most frequent adjacent pair.
  3. Merge that pair into a single new token. Add it to the vocabulary.
  4. Repeat thousands of times.

Frequent sequences (t+h → th, then th+e → the) get promoted into single tokens because they keep winning the "most frequent pair" contest. Rare sequences never do, so they stay fragmented. That's the whole trick — and it's why the tokenizer is trained, just like the model, but separately and first.

Anchor to what you know BPE is not gradient descent — no loss, no backprop. It's a greedy frequency-counting procedure run once, before training. The embedding table that turns each token ID into a vector is learned by backprop — but that's Lesson 2.

Why you actually care (the practical payoff)

Three real-world behaviors fall straight out of "the model sees tokens, not letters":

1. Cost and context are counted in tokens. APIs bill per token and the context window is a token budget, not a word or character count.2 English averages ~1.3 tokens/word; code, JSON, and non-English text are far less efficient — the same idea in some languages costs several times more tokens.

2. Models are bad at spelling and character counting. The infamous "how many r's in strawberry?" failure isn't stupidity — the model may see straw+berry as two opaque tokens. The individual letters are welded shut inside a token it never gets to look inside.1

3. Arithmetic and rare strings get weird. Numbers tokenize inconsistently (127 might be one token, 128 two), so digit-level math is fighting the representation. Rare words fragment, spending tokens and diluting meaning.

The one-sentence win

You can now explain, from first principles, why an LLM miscounts the letters in a word, why your bill is in tokens, and why non-English prompts cost more — all because the model reads BPE tokens, not text. That's the whole lesson.
